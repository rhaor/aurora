The Northern Lights

Welcome to the Aurora Borealis Explorer! Here you'll learn about one of nature's most spectacular light shows.

The northern lights, or aurora borealis, are a fascinating phenomenon that occurs when particles from the Sun interact with Earth's atmosphere.

Click through the sections to learn about each part of this amazing process!

The Sun's Role

The Sun constantly emits a stream of charged particles called the solar wind. These particles, mainly electrons and protons, travel through space at speeds of 300-800 kilometers per second!

Sometimes, the Sun releases huge bursts of energy called solar flares and coronal mass ejections (CMEs). These events send massive amounts of particles toward Earth, intensifying aurora displays.

The stronger the solar activity, the more spectacular the northern lights can be!

Earth's Magnetic Field

Earth's magnetic field acts like a giant shield, protecting us from the solar wind. This field is generated by the movement of molten iron in Earth's core.

The magnetic field is shaped like a donut, with field lines emerging from the South Pole and entering at the North Pole. Near the poles, these field lines are nearly vertical, creating a "funnel" effect.

This is why auroras are typically visible near Earth's magnetic poles - the particles are guided there by the magnetic field lines!

Particles & Colors

When solar particles reach Earth's atmosphere, they collide with gas molecules, causing them to become excited and release light.

Different gases create different colors:

  • Green: Oxygen at 60-150 km altitude (most common)
  • Red: Oxygen at higher altitudes (150-300 km)
  • Purple/Blue: Nitrogen
  • Pink: A mix of nitrogen and oxygen

Putting It All Together

Now we can see how it all works together:

  1. The Sun releases charged particles
  2. These particles travel to Earth via solar wind
  3. Earth's magnetic field guides them toward the poles
  4. The particles collide with atmospheric gases
  5. The excited gases release colorful light - the aurora!
